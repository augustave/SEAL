# SEAL — Imagery Codex

**Code:** `SEAL—IM`
**Status:** Canon
**Governs:** All photography, render, illustration, and diagram across all four palettes
**Version:** 1.0

---

## System premise

Anti-glint covers the material finish. This codex covers the image. They are not the same problem. A composition can obey every color rule and still fail doctrine because the photograph it carries was lit like a sneaker ad. Imagery is the second register — after color, the next-fastest signal a viewer reads.

Three constraints govern the entire image system:

1. **Documentary first, render second.** Every palette's primary image mode is documentary photography of real hardware in real conditions. Render is permitted but secondary, and only when the hardware is photo-impossible (under construction, in classified theater, in vacuum). Render that exists to glamorize is rejected.

2. **The light belongs to the theater, not the studio.** Each palette inherits a specific atmospheric and lighting register from its operational geography. Studio lighting — softboxes, key/fill/rim, smoke machines, controlled background — is rejected across all four palettes.

3. **The user is not the prop.** Operators, machinists, engineers, and crew are subjects, not models. Faces obscured where OPSEC or doctrinal humility applies. Equipment fits. The audience can tell when the depicted user has not done the work.

## Per-palette image doctrine

### SEAL—CW · Coldwater Pragmatism

**Light.** 900-lumen overcast. Sodium dock lights against black water. Red compartment lighting at night. Direct sun is rare; when it appears, low-angle Arctic light raking across haze grey topside. Golden hour is forbidden — there isn't one north of the GIUK gap in winter.

**Atmosphere.** Volumetric fog, sea spray, salt crust on housings, rain-streaked optics. Hull plating wet. The water itself is the only permitted reflective surface — it is environmental, not aesthetic.

**Subject framing.** Hardware mid-launch, mid-recovery, on-station. Shot from the gunwale, the dock, the bridge wing — never from a drone angle that wasn't available to the actual crew. Wide shots establish weather; macro shots establish materials (shackles, cable glands, bollards). The hull is the hero, not the operator.

**Geography of record.** Bath, Groton, Faslane, Barrow, Tromsø, Kiel, Reykjavík, Halifax. Never Palo Alto.

**Permitted render context.** Sub-surface UUV/AUV operating environments where photography is impossible. Sonar waterfall plots, bathymetric overlays, last-known-position ellipses. Render the data, not the hardware in dramatic vacuum.

**Rejected.** Studio renders with backlighting and lens flares. Drone hero-shots from impossible angles. Crew in dry, clean, well-lit conditions. Any composition that suggests the operator is comfortable.

### SEAL—RB · Rustbelt Kinetic

**Light.** 900-lux floor lighting at industrial color temperature. Sodium-vapor in the rafters. Quartz worklights on inspection benches. Coolant mist lit blue-green. Plasma torch arc as a punctuation light, never a key light. Third-shift conditions: real, working, occupied.

**Atmosphere.** Coolant mist, machining swarf, the specific haze of a high-bay running hot. Sparks from grinding cells. The line is the atmosphere — there is no separate "mood" beyond what the floor produces in the course of a shift.

**Subject framing.** Wide-angle deep-depth-of-field shots of the bay, vanishing into linear-perspective infinity (Sheeler / Bourke-White lineage). Rhythmic rows of robots, jigs, conveyors. Workers at heroic scale, shot slightly from below — Hine, not condescension, not parody. Lot numbers, serial plates, QC stamps visible and legible. **The product is rarely the hero. The line is the hero.** Fifty units in a jig means everything; one unit means nothing.

**Geography of record.** Detroit, Columbus, Toledo, Pittsburgh, Bethlehem, Tonawanda, Marinette, Decatur (AL), Huntsville. Plant and bay numbers visible where authentic.

**Permitted render context.** Factory layouts, supply-chain Sankey diagrams, takt-time charts, line-balance simulations. Render the system, not the unit posed in dramatic vacuum.

**Rejected.** Brooklyn-warehouse industrial chic — Edison bulbs, reclaimed wood, handlebar-mustached artisans. The garage tinkerer / hackerspace vibe. Empty-factory ruin porn (Packard Plant decay). Glamour shots of single units backlit on infinity-cove. Coastal condescension toward the workforce.

### SEAL—AN · Anechoic Minimal

**Light.** Single-source. Even, controlled, instrumentation-grade. The anechoic chamber lit by its own service lighting. Rack indicator LEDs as punctuation. The amber glow of vacuum-tube heaters. The phosphor green of a CRT trace. **The chamber is the canonical image — and it is the entire doctrine in one frame.**

**Atmosphere.** None. Air-conditioned, conditioned, isolated. No weather. No sky. No environment beyond the conditioned interior. Cable runs dressed and laced. Connectors as jewelry. The frame is enclosed and instrumented.

**Subject framing.** Anechoic chamber pyramidal-foam interior with single subject at center. AESA / phased-array faces shot dead-on, lattice as texture. Macro PCB, BGA, GaN die under microscope. Server rack faces, perfectly symmetric, every cable seated. **The hero shot is not the weapon — it is the plot of the weapon's effect on a Keysight or R&S analyzer screen.** Spectrograms, waterfall plots, polar gain patterns, S-parameter sweeps.

**Geography of record.** Any anechoic chamber. Any SCIF. Any rack room. Any fab cleanroom. The interiors are interchangeable — that is the point. There is no sky in this aesthetic.

**Permitted render context.** Phased-array beam patterns, EM propagation models, simulated jamming envelopes, GaN device-physics cross-sections. Render the field, not the weapon.

**Rejected.** Outdoor anything. Weather. People who are not seated at a console. Cyberpunk neon-grid-on-black. Steampunk vacuum-tube nostalgia. Glow-aesthetic gaming references. Any frame with a sky in it is misrouted from another palette.

### SEAL—DP · Desert Pragmatism

**Light.** Brutal and angular. Noon overhead with hard shadows, or low-angle golden hour with heat haze rippling the horizon. Sodium-vapor compound lighting at 0300 on a FOB. The orange sky behind a silhouetted operator with NVGs flipped up. **Heat haze is the Coldwater fog equivalent — the atmospheric distortion that signs the theater.**

**Atmosphere.** Dust kicked up by rotor wash. Sand visible on the bezel of a tablet. The specific buzz of a Group-1 fixed-wing drone overhead. Wind. Diesel. The breathing of a tired operator.

**Subject framing.** Documentary, helmet-cam, range-day. The operator rarely looks at camera. Equipment fits. Faces often obscured. Wide shots establish FOB context; close shots show wear, scuffs, sand, sweat. A Lattice tablet on a folding table, screen washed out by direct sunlight. A line of identical Switchblades on a tarp, propellants stenciled. The launcher tube on a shoulder. The IR strobe at fifty yards.

**Geography of record.** The CENTCOM brown band — Helmand, Anbar, Baluchistan, the Horn — extended to Niger, Somalia, the Southwest border, and INDOPACOM austere island contexts. Twentynine Palms range day is canon.

**Permitted render context.** Group-1/2 UAS in controlled airspace renders for technical documentation. Tactical operating picture (Lattice / ATAK lineage) with NATO symbology. Render the picture the operator sees, not the marketing of the picture.

**Rejected.** Silicon Valley product photography. Glossy renders. Soft pastel UI. Anything backlit and hero-shot in a studio with smoke machines. The mall-ninja tactical aesthetic — Magpul cosplay rather than Magpul. Plate-carrier-and-rifle-bro Instagram. The "future of warfare" register from VC brand films that have never seen a FOB.

### SEAL—AH · Apex Heraldic

**Light.** Pre-dawn ramp light at 0530 — sodium-vapor and tower lights against a paling eastern sky. Hangar interior at scale, lit by metal-halide high-bays. AOC plotting-floor light: dim ambient, screen-glow dominant, the controlled register of a 24/7 operations watch. High-altitude photography: thin-air sun, hard shadows, the curvature of Earth visible. The orange of contrails at sunset. Ceremonial daylight: an officer at a podium with the Air Force seal, a press-conference lighting setup that is *not* studio-glamorous but documentary-formal.

**Atmosphere.** Contrails. Vapor cones. Engine heat shimmer. The crisp transparency of high-altitude air. Below 30,000 ft: weather, cloud decks, the layer of haze visible only from above. Above 30,000 ft: the hard clarity that makes strategic photography legible. No fog, no dust, no coolant mist — these belong to other palettes. The atmospheric distortion is **the contrail**: long, geometric, chevron-forming, the visual signature that an air force is overhead.

**Subject framing.** Long-lens stabilized footage — the platform crosses the frame slowly. Wide hangar shots establishing scale: the B-21 in its bay at Plant 42, the B-52 in nuclear-alert configuration, the KC-46 with boom extended. Vertical climb shots showing altitude as authority. The chevron formation overhead. **The hero shot is altitude itself** — the curvature of Earth, the layered cloud below, the standoff distance made visible. Ceremonial framing: officer at podium with ribbon rack visible, change-of-command, retirement, unit-activation. The **ribbon rack** as a recurring visual primitive — close-up macro of a service-dress uniform showing campaigns earned. The **plotting floor** at night — large-screen ATO display, watch officers in shirt sleeves, the quiet of an operations center between events.

**Geography of record.** Plant 42 (Palmdale). Edwards AFB. Whiteman AFB. Ellsworth. McConnell. Hill. Eglin. Nellis. The Pentagon E-Ring. Al Udeid CAOC. Andersen AFB Guam. Hickam. Diego Garcia (where releasable). Wright-Patterson AFRL.

**Permitted render context.** Strategic platforms in classified configurations (RAM details, internal weapons bays, hypersonic propulsion cross-sections). Range-ring overlays on theater maps — combat radius unrefueled, with one tanker, with two. Standoff envelopes against adversary IADS. Mission-profile diagrams. Fuel-burn curves. Render the strategic geometry, not the platform in dramatic vacuum.

**Rejected.** Top Gun cockpit-camera. Maverick-style sortie photography. Helmet-cam from a fighter (different operator class than Desert's helmet-cam, both equally rejected here). Aviation lifestyle photography (chronograph watches on cockpit dashes, leather flight jackets posed in studio). The astronaut / orbital-launch register — that is Orbital, not yet drafted, and does not belong here. The vintage-warbird museum aesthetic. Glossy paint chipping in dramatic close-up — Air Force operational hardware is matte; the chrome brand pieces are class-A wool, not aircraft.

## Cross-palette image rules

1. **The light is the palette.** A warm-lit Coldwater is misrouted Desert. A cold-lit Desert is misrouted Coldwater. A skylit Anechoic is malformed. The atmospheric register is not interchangeable.

2. **The operator's frame, not the maker's.** Coldwater shows the USV on station, not the fitter who welded it. Desert shows the operator with the tablet, not the engineer who designed it. **Rustbelt inverts this rule** — Rustbelt shows the maker because Rustbelt's subject is the line. **Apex Heraldic also partially inverts this rule** — the operator (pilot, crew, AOC watch) is shown ceremonially as the *recipient of authority and decoration*, not in tactical action. The other palettes' operator-frame discipline still applies inside the tactical register.

3. **Documentary mode beats render mode.** When a real photograph exists, use it. Render is the fallback for the unreachable — sub-surface, in-vacuum, in-classified. A render that exists because the photograph would have been less glamorous is rejected on doctrinal grounds, not aesthetic preference.

4. **Heat haze, fog, void, dust, contrail.** One atmospheric distortion per palette, and they are not interchangeable. Coldwater carries fog. Rustbelt carries coolant mist and arc-flash haze. Anechoic carries no atmosphere — the void is the signature. Desert carries heat haze and dust. Apex Heraldic carries the contrail — long, geometric, chevron-forming.

5. **Stencils, plates, serial numbers visible.** Across all four palettes, hardware identifiers should be legible in the frame where authentic. Hull codes on Coldwater. Lot plates on Rustbelt. Connector designations on Anechoic. MIL-STD-130 nomenclature plates on Desert. The marking is provenance, not decoration.

## What this codex rejects

- **Studio backdrop infinity-cove product photography.** The hardware lives in a theater, not on a sweep.
- **Smoke machines, atmospheric haze added in post.** The atmospheres of the four palettes are real-world phenomena, not directorial flourishes.
- **Lifestyle operator photography.** The operator is not modeling a watch.
- **Drone hero-shots from angles the actual crew couldn't reach.** A camera position that wasn't operationally available is a marketing lie.
- **AI-generated imagery for hero contexts.** Permitted for diagram, schematic, and conceptual fill. Forbidden for hardware-in-theater hero shots — the lineage of authenticity in this brand is documentary, and a generated image breaks the chain.
- **Stock photography.** The same Getty image shows up in every defense-tech deck. None of it belongs here.
- **Neon, glow, lens flare, chromatic aberration as effect.** Optical artifacts that did not occur in the original capture are forbidden.
