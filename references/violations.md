# SEAL — Violations Codex

**Code:** `SEAL—VX`
**Status:** Canon
**Governs:** Identification and correction of doctrine violations across all four palettes
**Version:** 1.0

---

## System premise

The eight iron rules in `SKILL.md` define what SEAL is. This codex shows what SEAL is not. Each rule has a violation specimen (the kind of error a practitioner actually makes), a correction (the same intent, done in-doctrine), and a one-line diagnosis (the underlying error class).

Specimens are written, not rendered. Visual examples live in `examples/theater_demo.html`. The text specimens here are the canon — the demo is the illustration.

The principle: a brand violation is not a style error. It is a category error. Doctrine fails not because the colors are slightly off, but because the wrong thing is being said about the wrong subject.

## Iron rule violations

### Rule 1 — Mass over swagger

**Violation.**
Saronic landing-page hero copy: *"Saronic is reimagining the future of naval autonomy with cutting-edge unmanned platforms designed to empower the warfighter and revolutionize maritime operations."*

**Correction.**
*"Saronic. USV builder. Hull deliveries to USN. Texas-built, Bering-rated. Sea State 6 capable. 90 days on station."*

**Diagnosis.**
The first reads like a product launch. The work is heavier than that. Replace marketing verbs (reimagine, empower, revolutionize) with measurement verbs and named geographies. The voice should sound closer to a merchant marine log than a Series B announcement.

---

### Rule 2 — Anti-glint is doctrine

**Violation.**
Render of the Anduril Sentry tower with chrome reflections on the optic dome, a soft pastel-orange gradient sky behind it, ambient-occlusion glow under the base plate, and a JJ-Abrams lens flare across the sensor mast.

**Correction.**
The same tower photographed at Twentynine Palms during a noon range day. Hard overhead shadows. Dust on the base. Cerakote H-265 FDE flat across every painted surface. The only glint in the frame is the actual sun on the actual sensor glass — environmental, not finish.

**Diagnosis.**
Gloss is the visual signature of marketing. The brand is the hardware. Anything that exists in a render and not in the field is a tell.

---

### Rule 3 — One protected accent per palette

**Violation.**
A Coldwater interface using Safety Orange as the primary CTA color, the active-tab indicator, the alert badge, AND a decorative stripe on the wordmark. Four uses, four meanings.

**Correction.**
Safety Orange used exactly once: on the man-overboard alert state. Everything else in Anechoic Hull / Trench Navy / Haze Grey / Overcast White. The accent appears the moment something has gone wrong or someone needs to live — and not before.

**Diagnosis.**
The protected accent is a semantic class, not a brand color. Using it decoratively dilutes the signal. A user who sees orange in four contexts learns to ignore it; a user who sees it once learns it means rescue.

---

### Rule 4 — Real units only

**Violation.**
*"AI-powered, next-generation, blazing-fast loitering munition with world-class endurance and revolutionary precision-strike capability."*

**Correction.**
*"Switchblade 600. 11 lb launch weight. 40-minute loiter. 90 km range. 33 lb warhead-equivalent. $50K per unit at production rate."*

**Diagnosis.**
Marketing units (better, faster, smarter) are the tell of a brand that has no specs to show. Real units name the instrument that measured them: pounds, kilometers, minutes, dollars. The numbers do the persuading.

---

### Rule 5 — Cultural lineage required

**Violation.**
A proposed "Cyber Warfare" palette: black background, neon green accents, electric purple secondaries, glitch-typography display face. The accompanying brief describes the palette as "the aesthetic of offensive cyber operations." No cited heritage. No named operational platforms. No 30+ year industrial trace. Just vibes.

**Correction.**
A Cyber palette anchored in actual lineage: NSA *Cryptologic Quarterly* declassified covers (50-year typographic record), Bell Labs UNIX-era documentation, Schneier's *Applied Cryptography* layout, the early PGP / Cypherpunk-era visual record, the specific industrial design of high-assurance crypto hardware (Type 1 NSA-certified devices). The colors derive from the cited material — phosphor green from VT100, IBM 3270 amber, the muted greys of declassified document headers — not from gaming aesthetics.

**Diagnosis.**
A palette without lineage is a moodboard. The 30-year rule eliminates aesthetic cosplay without paperwork. If the palette can't trace its visual ancestors, it isn't doctrine — it's mood.

---

### Rule 6 — Stack-aware

**Violation.**
A standalone Anechoic Minimal brand book that never names the platforms carrying its payloads, the fabs producing its emitters, or the ground forces depending on its countermeasures. The palette presented as if EW happens in a closed room with no upstream supply chain and no downstream operational consumer.

**Correction.**
An Anechoic brief that opens with the stack relationship: *"Carried by Coldwater USVs into the GIUK gap. Fabbed in Rustbelt-doctrine GaN wafer lines in upstate New York. Protecting Desert ground forces operating across the brown band."* The brand's first paragraph names its dependencies and consumers.

**Diagnosis.**
The stack is the brand. A palette without its stack position is half a doctrine. Every palette is upstream or downstream of every other; the relationship is the credibility.

---

### Rule 7 — Reject the tourist palette

**Violation.**
A Coldwater USV company's pitch deck using FDE backgrounds with coyote-brown stencil typography and VS-17 cerise accents on every slide. The hardware is at sea, but the brand is dressed for Helmand. The deck looks like Anduril without being Anduril.

**Correction.**
The same company in Anechoic Hull black ground, Trench Navy surfaces, Haze Grey signature, Overcast White text, Safety Orange reserved for emergency UI states. Stencil retained — but only on hull markings in photography, never in slide typography. The deck reads as Bath, not Helmand.

**Diagnosis.**
FDE doesn't go to sea. The carrier's palette wins; never the tourist's. Borrowing another theater's signifiers is a category error: it tells the viewer the brand doesn't know which theater it belongs to.

---

### Rule 8 — One-line thesis on the cover

**Violation.**
A palette deck cover reading: *"Coldwater Pragmatism is the visual doctrine for defense-technology companies operating in the maritime autonomy domain, emphasizing operational endurance, autonomous capability in contested environments, and the harsh realities of the modern blue-water fight."*

**Correction.**
*"The ocean doesn't care about your DoD rating. Build like it."*

**Diagnosis.**
A paragraph hedges. A sentence commits. Doctrine commits. The cover is a discipline test: if the thesis can't compress to one sentence, the doctrine isn't finished.

## Common compound violations

These are violations that touch more than one rule. Most real-world failures are compound.

**The "tactical SaaS" failure** — Rules 1, 2, 4 simultaneously.
A defense-tech company writes Silicon Valley copy ("empowering the warfighter with AI-native autonomy"), renders glossy product shots on infinity-cove backdrops, and quotes performance in marketing units ("blazing fast"). Three rules broken in one frame.

**The "moodboard masquerade" failure** — Rules 5, 6, 8 simultaneously.
A new palette appears with no cited heritage, no stack position, and no thesis line. It is presented as inspirational mood imagery rather than enforceable doctrine. The deck is beautiful and operationally meaningless.

**The "everything is signal" failure** — Rule 3, repeated.
A brand uses its protected accent on the logo, the CTA, the alert state, the navigation, and decorative dividers. Every instance correct in isolation. Cumulatively the accent has been demoted from semantic class to decoration. The brand has trained users to ignore the one color that should mean *something is wrong*.

## How to use this codex

When reviewing any SEAL deliverable — a CODEX, a BRIEF, a slide cover, a UI mockup — walk through the eight rules in order. For each rule, ask: *what would the violation look like for this artifact?* If the artifact looks like the violation specimen, correct it before shipping. If it looks like the correction specimen, the rule holds.

This is not a checklist for first-pass creation. It is a checklist for final review. First-pass creation is governed by the codex entries (palette, type, color, image, coexistence). Final review is governed by this one.

---

*The wrong specimen is the most efficient teacher. Show the violation, show the correction, name the rule.*
